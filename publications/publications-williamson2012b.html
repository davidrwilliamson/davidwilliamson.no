Modelling the locomotion nervous system in the nematode C. elegans: a developmental perspective

    Abstract

    In this research project I use computer simulation and experimental evidence to investigate the effects of both differences in body shape and age on locomotion in C. elegans. Taking a robust computational model for forward locomotion in the adult wildtype (standard laboratory strain) worm, I extend the model to include a juvenile stage in the worm’s development with realistic body size, mutant worms with long or short bodies(specifically, strains lon-2, dpy-1 and dpy-11 ), and a worms of a range of ages ranging from early adulthood to old age. The model is tested for robustness, and I perform analysis on data obtained experimentally through trials with both mutant and ageing worms, and draw comparisons with predictions made from simulation results.

    I show that the model used is capable of simulating a wide range of bodyshapes and has useful predictive power in simulating the locomotion of unusually shaped worms. Using rate of undulation as a measure of effective locomotion, I find that worms either proportionally longer or shorter than the wildtype produce slower undulation, and shorter worms in particular are less capable of good movement. This is likely directly connected to ratio of the worm’s diameter to its length, which influences its undulation waveform and the environmental forces to which it is subjected.

    I demonstrate that ageing related loss of mobility in C. elegans can be well modelled in early adulthood, and suggest that loss of muscle power alone can account for the majority of this change. However the elasticity of muscle fibres also plays a partial role, and the evidence suggests that this may become an important factor in older worms.
