Learning from Digital Collections: Digitisation and Machine Learning for Large-Scale Museum Studies

    Abstract

    The world's museums collectively preserve billions of natural history specimens: identified by experts, curated, and verified. These specimens provide unique and irreplaceable historical data that can help to answer our most pressing questions relating to climate change, changes in phenology, habitat and species range, and biodiversity loss. Large-scale studies incorporating museum collections are vital to understanding these topics, but unlocking the information in collections typically requires physical access to the specimens and careful examination by experts. For the large-scale studies needed to understand global changes, involving millions of specimens across many nations, this is impractical. It is therefore increasingly important for museums to provide digital representations of specimens, comprising images and associated metadata, which can be accessed by researchers anywhere on the planet through searchable online databases. Digital specimens and modern computational analysis allow studies to be conducted at geographic and temporal scales that were previously impossible. We present two case studies demonstrating the value and limitations of such an approach.
